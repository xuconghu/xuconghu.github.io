ABOUT ME

Hi! I'm Xucong, a first-year master's student in Psychology at Zhejiang University, working in the Social Cognition Lab with Prof. Jifan Zhou. I'm on track to graduate in June 2027. Before starting my master's, I earned my B.Sc. in Psychology from Southwest University in Chongqing, China. During undergrad, I also worked as a research assistant in several labs at the Chinese Academy of Sciences and Tsinghua University, where I enjoyed turning curiosity into hands-on research.

As a psychology student, my interests may be a bit unusual. Deeply influenced by Dr. Xuan Zhao, I am particularly interested in Human-Robot Interaction (HRI), with a focus on designing social robots that can collaborate as naturally as humans do. Grounded in human-specific social cognitive capacities—like theory of mind, perspective-taking, and empathy—I conduct user-centered research to explore how we can design robots that people easily believe can understand and interact with them. My goal is to offer design principles and create robots that bystanders can instantly understand and fall in love with.

By the way, I am also a heavy sci-fi enthusiast. I love franchises like Star Wars, Alien, Gundam, and I enjoy building LEGO and Bandai Gundam models in my spare time. I also enjoy singing—anything from pop to hip-hop—and I'm a gamer at heart, with some of my happiest hours spent climbing ranks in Valorant, League of Legends, and Teamfight Tactics.

Good to see you R2-D2: Inducing spontaneous perspective-taking towards non-human agents through human-like gaze and reach

Xucong Hu, Haokui Xu, Hui Chen, Mowei Shen, Jifan Zhou
#JOURNAL #Cognition 2025
Citations: Loading...

Abstract

Observing the world from another's perspective is a fundamental social cognitive ability essential for human cooperation. With the increasing prevalence of intelligent systems in our society, highly intelligent social robots such as R2-D2 in Star Wars is becoming a reality, thus it is compelling to explore how this capability can extend from humans to non-human agents. Although previous research indicates that a human-like appearance might facilitate this extension, our study contends that human-like actions are more critical. We conducted four experiments involving agents that did not resemble humans but could perform two human-like actions: reach and gaze, which exhibited the perceptual and behavioral abilities that were essential for social interaction. The experiments found that agents prompted spontaneous perspective-taking among participants when they displayed both actions. Importantly, perspective-taking was maintained only when gaze preceded reach, underscoring the causal relationship that behavior should be interpreted as the consequence of perception. These results highlight the importance of human-like actions rather than mere appearance in fostering spontaneous perspective-taking towards non-human agents, providing insights for improving human-agent interaction.
